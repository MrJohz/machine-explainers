Unbreakable Cyphers:

How To Build An Engima Machine

Here's the problem in a nutshell: human writing is really predictable.

The simplest possible cypher takes your text and rotates all the letters through the alphabet by a fixed amount. We can call this amount the key, and for the sake of convenience, our key will always be a letter from A–Z. So if our key is C, we'll rotate each letter from our input text by three characters (because C is the third letter of the alphabet).

But this doesn't really change the input text all that much — sure, all the letters are now different, but because we know what human writing ought to look like, we can make some reasonable guesses as to what the cypher text actually is.

Here's a demonstration. On the left is some text, and a key. On the right is the output after we've passed the text through our very simple cypher. Below that, are two charts — one shows the distribution of letters in our original text, and the other shows how they're distributed after being encrypted.
